💡 OPC Entrepreneurship Practical Advice

Essential Difference Between OPC and Freelancers

Freelancer: Essentially selling personal time, charging by hour/project, no income when there’s no order, scale highly dependent on personal energy.

One-Person Company (OPC): Essentially building a “small business system” where products/services are reusable, income can be scaled, and revenue can be generated even when you’re offline.

Simply Put: A one-person company is packaging “yourself” as a micro-enterprise to operate. You are both CEO, product manager, and sales, but through tools and systems, you enable “one person to produce like a company.”

Three Reasons Why OPC Became Massively Feasible in 2026

  1. AI Tools Make “One Person Acting as Multiple People” Possible

    AI has evolved from “helping you write a paragraph” to “helping you complete an entire task”:

    • Coding: From code completion → AI autonomously completing full feature development (Claude Code, Cursor)
    • Marketing: From generating a few copywriting snippets → One-click output of complete publishing materials (A9 AI and other marketing Agents)
    • Research: From search result lists → AI directly providing research reports with citations (Perplexity)
    • Finance: From manual bookkeeping → AI automatic classification + report generation + tax filing suggestions

  2. SaaS Tools at Cabbage Prices

    Many efficient SaaS tools offer free or low-price tiers, reducing entrepreneurship costs.

  3. Mature Digital Platform Ecosystem

    WeChat, Douyin, Taobao, and other platforms provide complete infrastructure for traffic, payments, and logistics.

Real Case: Independent Developer Earns Tens of Thousands Monthly

Case: Google Indexing Acceleration Tool

  • Product: A tool that forces Google to speed up indexing of your pages. After submitting a URL, it helps you handle sitemap submission API, allowing Google to crawl your pages faster and thus obtain SEO rankings sooner.
  • Origin: The founder had made 20+ products. Buying external indexing tools for each would be too expensive, so they wrote an internal tool. At the beginning, there wasn’t even a UI—just a button.
  • Turning Point: Later, they felt this was worth running as an experiment, so the “internal tool” became a public product.
  • Insight: Solving your own pain points → Internal tool → Public product is a common productization path for independent developers.
